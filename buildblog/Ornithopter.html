Radio-Controlled Ornithopter – First Prototype

January 2026

This build explores the feasibility of a mechanically simple, radio-controlled ornithopter focused on flapping efficiency rather than sustained endurance.

1. Context and Objective

The goal of this project was to validate whether a lightweight flapping mechanism could generate sufficient lift at low Reynolds numbers using off-the-shelf RC components.

2. Assumptions and Constraints

Engineering Assumption: The wings are modeled as rigid flat plates with no active twist control. This simplifies the mechanism but underestimates aerodynamic efficiency.
  • Total mass target: < 250 g
  • Single motor, no gearbox
  • Indoor flight envelope

3. Build and Implementation

The flapping mechanism uses a crank-slider driven directly by a brushed DC motor. Wing spars are carbon rods with heat-shrink skin.

Crank-slider mechanism prior to wing installation.

4. Analysis and Observations

At peak motor speed, the flapping frequency reached approximately 6 Hz. Significant torsional oscillation was observed in the wing spars, indicating under-designed stiffness.

Key Insight: Structural compliance dominated aerodynamic effects, masking any lift gains from increased flapping frequency.

5. Results

  • Short hops achieved (< 1 second)
  • No sustained flight
  • Mechanism reliability acceptable

6. Lessons Learned and Next Steps

Future iterations will prioritize wing torsional stiffness and introduce passive twist via compliant joints rather than rigid spars.
